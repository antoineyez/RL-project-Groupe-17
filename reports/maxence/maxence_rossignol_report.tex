\section{Personal Contribution}

My main contributions to the project focused on the \textbf{training infrastructure}, \textbf{experimental pipeline}, and \textbf{cluster deployment}. Specifically:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Fair comparison framework}: I converted the DQN training loop from an episode-based to a \textbf{timestep-based} interface, so that both our DQN and the Stable-Baselines3 (SB3) DQN are trained for the same number of environment steps. This ensures the training curves share the same x-axis and the comparison is transparent.
    \item \textbf{Incremental result saving}: I implemented CSV-based logging that saves training rewards and evaluation results after each seed. This allows partial runs to still produce usable data---a design choice that proved critical when our first cluster job was killed mid-training.
    \item \textbf{DCE cluster deployment}: I set up the full training pipeline on CentraleSup\'elec's distributed computing cluster (SLURM-based), including conda environment creation with CUDA 12.4 support, batch job scripts with configurable parameters, and monitoring.
    \item \textbf{Debugging and fixes}: I identified and fixed several issues in the pipeline, including a missing \texttt{Monitor} wrapper for SB3 reward logging, an observation space mismatch caused by \texttt{configure()} requiring a \texttt{reset()} before \texttt{DummyVecEnv} construction, and checkpoint saving during training rather than only at the end.
\end{itemize}

\section{Experiments}

All experiments use the instructor-provided configuration: \texttt{highway-v0} with Kinematics observations ($10 \times 5$ matrix), \texttt{DiscreteMetaAction} (5 actions), 45 vehicles, 4 lanes, 30-second episodes, and normalized rewards.

Both agents share the same core hyperparameters for a fair comparison:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Learning rate: $10^{-3}$, discount factor $\gamma = 0.99$, batch size 64
    \item Replay buffer: 50{,}000 transitions, target network update every 500 steps
    \item Epsilon: linear decay from 1.0 to 0.05 over 50\% of training (DQN) / 30\% (SB3)
    \item Network: MLP $50 \to 256 \to 256 \to 5$ for both agents (we set SB3's \texttt{policy\_kwargs=dict(net\_arch=[256, 256])} to match our architecture exactly)
\end{itemize}

An earlier run (v2) used a smaller default \texttt{MlpPolicy} ($64 \to 64$) for SB3 while our DQN used $256 \to 128$, making the comparison unfair. In the final run (v3), we matched both networks to $256 \to 256$ and used seeds 52, 53, 54.

I ran experiments on the DCE cluster (NVIDIA GTX 1080 Ti). A first attempt with 3 seeds was killed by SLURM after exceeding the 5-hour time limit. The incremental CSV saving ensured no data was lost for the completed seed. This led us to switch to the \texttt{gpu\_prod\_long} partition (48h limit), where the full run---40{,}000 timesteps $\times$ 3 seeds (52, 53, 54) $\times$ 2 agents---completed successfully.

Key observations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Our DQN reaches high reward levels quickly within the first $\sim$5{,}000 timesteps but continues to exhibit noisy, high-variance training throughout, with frequent reward drops caused by crash episodes.
    \item SB3's learning is delayed by its \texttt{learning\_starts=1000} parameter, but once it begins learning, it converges to a very \textbf{stable and consistent} policy with near-zero variance across seeds.
    \item Both agents converge toward similar mean performance ($\sim$20) by the end of training, but SB3's final policy is significantly more stable.
\end{itemize}

With matched architectures, SB3 now outperforms our DQN on stability metrics: higher mean reward (20.48 vs 19.85), dramatically lower variance (0.05 vs 4.63), and \textbf{zero crashes} across all 150 evaluation episodes. This reverses our earlier results (v2), where SB3 used a smaller $64 \times 64$ network and performed significantly worse---highlighting how \textbf{architecture parity is essential for fair comparison}.

\subsection{Failure Analysis}

Our DQN's 29 crash episodes follow two patterns:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Early crashes} (steps 4--8, e.g., reward 2.16 at step 4): the agent attempts a lane change immediately after reset into an occupied lane. These represent catastrophic misjudgments of the initial traffic configuration and produce very low rewards.
    \item \textbf{Late crashes} (steps 19--30, e.g., reward 20.97 at step 30): the agent survives most of the episode but collides near the end, typically during a lane change in dense traffic. These episodes still accumulate a near-full reward before the crash.
\end{itemize}

SB3's policy, by contrast, exhibits \textbf{zero crashes}. Its near-constant reward of $\sim$20.45 suggests it learned a highly conservative strategy---likely maintaining a steady speed and avoiding lane changes entirely. While safe, this policy never reaches the higher rewards (22--25) that our DQN occasionally achieves through more aggressive driving.

\section{What Worked and What Failed}

\textbf{What worked:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item The timestep-based unification made the comparison genuinely fair and the training curves directly readable on the same x-axis.
    \item Incremental CSV saving paid off immediately when our first cluster job timed out. Without it, 2+ hours of compute would have been lost entirely.
    \item Matching network architectures (v2 $\to$ v3) was critical: SB3's default $64 \times 64$ \texttt{MlpPolicy} gave misleadingly poor results. Once we set both agents to $256 \times 256$, SB3 demonstrated its strength---its carefully tuned training loop produces more stable policies than our from-scratch implementation.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{What failed:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Unfair architecture comparison (v2):} our initial results showed our DQN ``outperforming'' SB3, but SB3 was using a network $4\times$ smaller. This is a cautionary tale about controlling all variables in ML experiments.
    \item \textbf{SB3 reward logging}: the \texttt{RewardLoggerCallback} silently produced empty lists because the environment was not wrapped in a \texttt{Monitor}. The fix was a single line (\texttt{Monitor(env)}).
    \item \textbf{Compute budget estimation}: I underestimated how long 40{,}000 timesteps $\times$ 3 seeds would take on the cluster ($\sim$14 hours), leading to the first job being killed at the SLURM time limit.
    \item \textbf{Observation space bug}: SB3's \texttt{DummyVecEnv} reads the observation space at construction time, before \texttt{configure()} takes effect. The fix was to call \texttt{env.reset()} after \texttt{configure()} so the observation space updates from $(5, 5)$ to $(10, 5)$.
\end{itemize}

\section{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{No hyperparameter tuning} was performed. Our DQN's 19\% crash rate could likely be reduced by tuning the exploration schedule or adding safety-oriented reward shaping. SB3 benefits from its well-tuned training loop (gradient clipping, learning rate scheduling, etc.) which our implementation lacks.
    \item \textbf{SB3's conservative policy}: SB3 achieves zero crashes but with near-constant reward ($\sim$20.45), suggesting it avoids lane changes entirely. A more nuanced analysis would require logging per-step actions to confirm this hypothesis.
    \item The \textbf{extension task} has not yet been completed. Future work could explore Double-DQN and Prioritized Experience Replay as natural improvements to the current DQN implementation.
    \item The failure analysis is qualitative. A more rigorous approach would involve recording the full observation trajectory leading to each crash to identify systematic failure patterns.
\end{itemize}
